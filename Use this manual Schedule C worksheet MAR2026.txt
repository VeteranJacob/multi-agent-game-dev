Copy it into Word, Excel, or a notebook and fill it in from your bank statements.

Use this formula:

**Deductible Amount = Amount Paid × Business %**

Examples:

* $120 phone bill × 60% business use = $72 deductible
* $300 office chair × 100% business use = $300 deductible

# 2) Category guide for Schedule C

Use these labels in your log.

## Common Schedule C categories

**Advertising**
Business cards, flyers, online ads, website promotion

**Car and truck expenses**
Business vehicle costs if you are using actual expenses

**Commissions and fees**
Referral fees, sales commissions, platform commissions

**Contract labor**
Payments to freelancers or subcontractors

**Insurance**
Business insurance only

**Interest**
Business loan interest, business credit card interest

**Legal and professional services**
Lawyer, accountant, tax preparer for business return, bookkeeping

**Office expense**
Postage, office admin expenses, routine office items

**Rent or lease**
Office rent, equipment lease

**Repairs and maintenance**
Repair of business equipment or business property

**Supplies**
Paper, toner, packaging, small consumable items

**Taxes and licenses**
Business license, permit fees, payroll taxes paid by employer

**Travel**
Hotels, airfare, business trips away from home

**Meals**
Business meals, only deductible portion

**Utilities**
Business phone, business internet, business electricity

**Other expenses**
Software, bank fees, merchant fees, domain names, cloud storage, education related to current business

# 3) Simple rules for classifying bank statement items

## Usually easy to place

* Office Depot, Staples → Supplies or Office expense
* Adobe, Microsoft, QuickBooks, Zoom → Other expenses or Office expense
* Bank monthly fee → Other expenses
* Stripe, Square, PayPal fees → Other expenses
* Attorney or CPA payment → Legal and professional services
* Business license fee → Taxes and licenses
* Facebook Ads, Google Ads → Advertising

## Needs more review

* Amazon, Walmart, Target → depends on what you bought
* Gas stations → vehicle expense only if business driving
* Restaurants → meals only if business-related
* Cell phone bill → prorate business %
* Internet bill → prorate business %
* Home utility bill → usually not directly on Schedule C unless tied to home office rules
* Cash withdrawals → only count if you have solid backup records

## Do not treat as Schedule C expenses

* Personal groceries
* Personal rent
* Owner draws
* Credit card payments themselves
* Loan principal
* Federal income tax
* Personal child support
* Personal transfers between accounts

# 6) Manual month-by-month method

For each bank statement month:

## Step 1

Go line by line and mark each transaction:

* B = business
* P = personal
* S = split
* N = not deductible

## Step 2

Write only B and S items into the expense log.

## Step 3

For S items, calculate only the business part.

## Step 4

Add each item into the proper category total.

## Step 5

At the end of each month, total the categories.

That way year-end is just adding up 12 monthly sheets.

# 8) Special caution areas

## Vehicle

Pick one method and stay consistent:

* standard mileage, or
* actual expenses

Do not mix them incorrectly.

## Equipment

If you bought a laptop, printer, furniture, or major equipment, do not automatically throw it into supplies. It may need separate treatment.

## Inventory

If you sell products, purchases for resale may belong in cost of goods sold, not regular expenses.

## Mixed accounts

If your personal and business spending are mixed in one bank account, be extra careful to separate personal items out.

# 9) Best manual filing workflow

1. Gather all bank statements
2. Gather receipts and invoices
3. Build the transaction log
4. Total by category
5. Fill the Schedule C summary sheet
6. Transfer totals to the tax form
7. Keep the worksheet with your records

# 10) Fast paper-ledger version

If you do not want a spreadsheet, make one page for each category:

* Advertising
* Office expense
* Supplies
* Utilities
* Legal and professional
* Travel
* Meals
* Other expenses

Then write:

* date
* vendor
* amount
* purpose

Add the total at the bottom of each page.

That is the old-school manual bookkeeping method.
